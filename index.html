Reading how cells breathe — one channel at a time.

I study how cells sense carbon dioxide — building genetically encoded fluorescent tools and uncovering the connexin hemichannels that translate CO₂ into biological signals. After a PhD in CO₂ signalling and postdoctoral work across Warwick and Osaka, I'm now training in medicine at the University of Surrey, bridging molecular physiology and clinical practice.

About

A molecular biologist becoming a clinician — trained to ask how the body senses its own chemistry, and now learning to care for the people that chemistry keeps alive.

Born in Constanța, Romania, I read Biomedical Science at the University of Warwick, graduating with First Class Honours before joining the MRC Doctoral Training Partnership. My PhD, supervised by Professor Nicholas Dale, asked a deceptively simple question: how do cells detect carbon dioxide? Answering it meant designing genetic tools to measure CO₂ and hunting for the receptors that respond to it.

Along the way I designed, cloned and mutated more than 75 constructs, ran patch-clamp electrophysiology on connexin hemichannels, and screened roughly ten million cells on Osaka's AMATERAS giga-pixel microscope to engineer new fluorescent biosensors. My work spanned a Wellcome Trust scholarship and fellowships from the MRC and JSPS; it produced first-author papers in Communications Biology and eLife.

In 2025 I began Graduate Entry Medicine at the University of Surrey, with the desire of moving science from bench to bedside. Outside the lab I've played guitar for seventeen years and hold a black belt in Shotokan karate — both, in their way, exercises in patient, precise practice.

Research

Where the work lives

01

CO₂ Sensing & Connexin Hemichannels

Certain β-connexins — and, as my recent work in eLife shows, the α-connexin Cx43 — open directly in response to carbon dioxide through a carbamylation motif. Using dye-loading assays and whole-cell patch clamp, I map how CO₂ gates these channels and drives ATP release.

02

Genetically Encoded Fluorescent Biosensors

I design and evolve fluorescent proteins into sensors for otherwise invisible signals (CO₂). Screening millions of cells on the AMATERAS or FACS, single-cell picking and sequencing for the best-performing variants.

03

The Evolution of CO₂ Signalling

By comparing connexins across 400 million years of evolution, I traced how the CO₂-sensing hemichannel arose — showing that losing Cx26's C-terminal tail was the innovation that made breathing-related chemosensitivity possible.

04

Volatile Diagnostics & the Electronic Nose

Diseases leave a chemical signature in the air. I wrote an article on electronic-nose platforms, exploring how volatile organic compounds and smell profiles could diagnose urinary tract infection — a review later selected as an Editor's Choice article.
